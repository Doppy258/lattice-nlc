PRD: Lattice
1. Product Overview
Product Name
Lattice
One-Line Description
Lattice helps users find local offers by creating structured requests, then matching them with nearby small
businesses that fit their budget, timing, distance, and preferences.
Core Concept
Users do not scroll endlessly through local business listings. They create a structured “Lattice” describing what
they need, and the app matches them with relevant local business offers.
Example:
“I need lunch after school, under $15, within 3 km, open between 3:30 PM and 5:00 PM.”
Lattice returns ranked local business offers, explains why each offer matched, lets users claim/save offers,
verifies redemption, then unlocks reviews and personal ranking.
Competition Goal
Build a standalone application for the FBLA Coding & Programming event that demonstrates strong
technical skill, clear UX, full prompt alignment, meaningful data analysis, intelligent matching, input
validation, and polished presentation readiness.
2. Problem Statement
User Problem
People often want to support local businesses but do not want to waste time comparing dozens of listings,
ratings, menus, prices, and hours. Existing tools show what exists nearby, but they do not directly match a
user’s specific need to local business offers.
1
Business Problem
Small local businesses struggle to reach nearby customers at the exact moment those customers are ready
to buy. They may have slow hours, unused appointments, expiring inventory, or student discounts that
customers never discover.
Product Opportunity
Lattice creates a two-sided local discovery tool:
1.
2.
3.
4.
Users describe what they need.
Local businesses publish structured offers.
The app matches the two sides using a scoring algorithm.
Claims, redemptions, reviews, bookmarks, and reports create measurable local support.

4. Product Positioning
Positioning Statement
Lattice is a local offer-matching app that helps users discover and support small businesses by turning specific
user needs into verified, claimable local offers.
What Makes Lattice Different
Most business discovery tools are built around browsing listings. Lattice is built around structured demand.
The user does not start with:
“Show me all restaurants near me.”
The user starts with:
3
“I need lunch under $15 after school.”
The product then matches that need with real local offers.
Main User Benefit
Lattice saves users time and money by turning a specific need into a short ranked list of local offers they can
claim immediately.
Main Business Benefit
Lattice helps small businesses reach customers with timely offers and gives them analytics on views, claims,
redemptions, reviews, and customer interest.
5. Product Principles
1. Structured, Not Chaotic
Users should not be able to post absurd freeform requests like:
“I want a haircut for free within 15 minutes.”
The request flow must use structured inputs, preset ranges, semantic validation, and category-specific
rules.
2. Minimal Visual Design
The app should look clean, simple, and modern. Avoid clutter. The interface should feel easy enough for a
judge to understand within seconds.
3. Clear Rubric Alignment
Every major feature should visibly support the FBLA topic or rating sheet:

4. Standalone and Demo-Safe
The app must run without internet. Use seeded local data. Do not depend on live APIs, real maps,
payments, SMS, or external authentication.
5. Explainable Intelligence
The app should not just say “best match.” It should explain why an offer was ranked highly.
Example:
“This offer fits your budget, is 1.8 km away, is open after school, has a verified 4.7 rating, and
includes a student deal.”

13.1 Match Scoring Algorithm
Purpose
Rank offers by how well they fit a Lattice request.
Formula
Score =
0.25 times category score
•
•
•
•
•
•
0.20 times budget score
0.15 times distance score
0.15 times rating score
0.10 times time score
0.10 times verification score
0.05 times preference score
Each subscore is from 0 to 100.
Score Fields
Category score:
•
•
•
100 if offer category matches request category
60 if related category
0 if unrelated
Budget score:
•
100 if offer price is within budget
36
•
•
•
70 if offer price is within 15 percent above budget
30 if offer price is above budget but has strong rating
0 if far above budget
Distance score:
•
•
•
100 if business is within selected distance
Scales down as distance approaches max
0 if outside max distance
Rating score:
•
•
Convert average rating from 1 to 5 into 0 to 100
Example: 4.5 rating becomes 90
Time score:
•
•
•
100 if offer and business are available during request window
50 if partially available
0 if unavailable
Verification score:
•
•
•
100 if business is verified and offer requires redemption
70 if business is verified
40 if unverified
Preference score:
•
Add points for matching preferences like student discount, open now, group-friendly, accessible,
quiet, vegetarian, etc.
Match Reasons
Generate human-readable reasons from score components.

13.5 Pairwise Ranking Algorithm
Purpose
Rank businesses based on user comparison, rather than only 1 to 5 ratings.
39
Trigger
After review submission.
Logic
Use binary insertion.
Input:
•
•
•
Existing ranked list
New business
Comparison answers
Process: 1. Compare new business to middle item. 2. If user says new business is better, search upper half.
3. If worse, search lower half. 4. Repeat until insertion point is found. 5. Insert new business into ranked list.
Complexity
Linear insertion may require up to n comparisons.
Binary insertion requires about log base 2 of n comparisons.
Example: If the user has 64 ranked businesses, binary insertion takes about 6 comparisons.
UX Rule
Limit demo comparison to 3 prompts unless user chooses to continue.
13.6 Report Calculations
User estimated savings:
•
Sum of originalPrice minus price for redeemed offers where originalPrice exists.
Business conversion rate:
•
Redeemed claims divided by offer views.
Claim redemption rate:
•
Average rating:
Redeemed claims divided by total claims.
•
Sum of ratings divided by review count.

22. Q&A Prep
Why did you choose this idea?
Because the prompt asks us to help users discover and support small local businesses. We wanted to go
beyond a static directory and create a tool that matches real customer needs with timely local offers.
How is this different from Google Maps?
Google Maps is built around searching for places. Lattice is built around structured local requests and offer
matching. Users describe what they need, and the app ranks claimable offers based on fit, budget, distance,
timing, ratings, and preferences.
What is the intelligent feature?
the matching engine. It scores and ranks offers using category fit, budget, distance, rating, time availability,
verification, and preferences. It also explains why each offer matched.
How do you prevent absurd requests?
The app uses structured fields and semantic validation. Each need type has realistic budget rules, required
fields, cooldowns, and validation checks.
53
How do you prevent bot activity?
Users complete a mock verification step before matching, claim codes are generated, claims have
cooldowns, reviews require redemption, and duplicate claims are blocked.
What data structures did you use?
Arrays and typed objects store businesses, offers, users, requests, claims, reviews, saved items, and
rankings. Personal rankings use ordered arrays, and the app uses binary insertion for efficient pairwise
ranking.
Why use binary insertion?
When placing a new business into a user’s ranking, binary insertion reduces comparisons from linear time
to logarithmic time. This improves UX because the user answers fewer comparison questions.
What reports does the app generate?
Users can view savings, claims, redemptions, businesses supported, favorite categories, and rating
patterns. Businesses can view offer views, claims, redemptions, conversion rate, ratings, and review tags.

25. Final Product Summary
Lattice is a structured local offer-matching app for small businesses. Users create a specific request, the app
validates it, ranks local offers using the matching engine, allows users to claim verified offers, and turns redemptions
into trusted reviews, personal rankings, and reports. Businesses can publish offers, redeem claim codes,
and analyze customer engagement.
The product is designed to clearly satisfy the FBLA Coding & Programming prompt while giving judges a
strong demonstration of UX, logic, data structures, validation, reports, and intelligent matching.
